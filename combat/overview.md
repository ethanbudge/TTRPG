# Conflict Overview

The Party has one conflict system. A cellar knife-fight, a shouted parliamentary ambush, a three-month trade war, and a fleet action in a debris field are all the same procedure — **beats, goals, contests, tracks** — at different zoom levels. [Personal Conflict](combat/individual.md) and [Faction Conflict](combat/faction.md) add scale-specific texture; this page is the engine they share.

## When to Enter Conflict

Enter structured conflict when **more than one actor wants incompatible things urgently** and the order of events matters. Otherwise, stay in free scene play with ordinary contests. Leaving conflict is just as important: the moment one side's will is broken — tracks filled, conditions decisive, objective settled — stop counting beats and return to fiction.

## Timescale — Set the Clockspeed

At the start of a conflict the GM declares the **beat length** — what one round of the table represents:

| Clockspeed | One beat is… | For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Blitz** | seconds | brawls, firefights, boarding actions |
| **Operation** | minutes–hours | heists, manhunts, sieges-in-progress |
| **Season** | days–weeks | trade wars, election campaigns, blockades |
| **Era** | months+ | generational struggles, cold wars |

Nothing else changes. A goal at Blitz speed is *"I drag the captain behind the barricade"*; the same player's goal at Season speed is *"I flood their harbor with cheap grain until their merchants defect."* Same dice, same outcomes, same tracks.

## Beats & the Spotlight

Each beat, **every actor gets one goal** — each PC, each significant NPC or rival asset. There is no initiative count:

- The table chooses order by fiction — who's moving *now*? When in doubt, go around the table, players first.
- The GM's significant actors take their goals woven between player goals, where the fiction puts them. Mooks and hazards mostly don't take goals at all — they act through **stakes**, arriving as the cost of player losses and ties.
- A goal is **one sentence**. If a turn is taking more than a couple of minutes, the GM asks for the sentence, sets the dice, and rolls. That tempo commitment — declare, roll, narrate, next — is the whole cure for the slow-combat disease.

**Goals, not moves.** Declare outcomes you want (*"I want their flank commander dead or fleeing"*), never mechanics (*"I attack twice"*). The table maps the sentence to a stat, edges, and stakes. Anything a person could try, a goal can attempt — disarm, humiliate, stampede the cattle, hold the door, buy time, change the subject of the war.

## Distance — The Four Bands

Space is measured in four **range bands**, sized relative to the arena:

| Band | Knife fight | City operation | Fleet action | Trade war |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Contact** | grappling | same room | boarding / docked | same market, face to face |
| **Close** | same room | same block | gun range / same orbit | same city |
| **Far** | across the hall | across the district | same system, hours away | same region |
| **Distant** | out of the fight | across the city | other systems | across the world |

- Moving **one band** is free alongside your goal if nothing opposes it; if something does, the movement *is* contested.
- Moving **two or more bands** is your whole goal for the beat.
- Tools, weapons, and assets carry **range tags** — a blade works at Contact, a rifle at Close/Far, a broadside at Close, a propaganda press at Distant. Range tags are permissions, not bonuses: they define which goals you can declare from where.

## Tags

A short, closed list of descriptive tags covers what other games do with equipment tables. Items, assets, and features may carry:

| Tag | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Piercing** | Ignores protection tags |
| **Sweeping** | The goal may target a cluster (a squad, a crowd, a formation) |
| **Silent** | Wins don't raise alarms |
| **Anchored** | Can't change bands (emplacements, fortresses, institutions) |
| **Long** | Only usable at Far or Distant (artillery, torpedoes, market manipulation) |
| **Protection** | Negate 1 incoming strain, once per scene ([details](core/strain-and-conditions.md#protection)) |

That's the entire equipment system. Anything more belongs in fiction.

## Build-Up & Payoff

The combo engine — how snappy turns still produce crescendos:

- **Set-up goals.** Any win can be *banked as an edge for a named ally* instead of taken as effect, if the fiction connects ("I pin them down — go!"). Banked edges expire at scene's end.
- **Strong wins** (margin 5+) hand out boons — extra strain, positions, conditions, clock ticks.
- **Conditions are combos.** *Blinded* on the duelist, then the disarm. *Discredited* on the minister, then the vote. The payoff for teamwork isn't bigger numbers — it's changing what the situation *is* before the decisive roll.
- **Clocks in conflict.** Objectives beyond "drop them" get clocks: *Overload the Reactor (4)*, *The Vault Opens (6)*, *Their Coalition Splits (8)*. Fights with clocks in them end by objective, not annihilation — usually faster, always more interesting.

## Zooming

Conflicts change scale mid-stream; the system follows without switching rules:

- **Zoom out** when faction resources commit or scale actors join — the duel spills into a riot, the boarding action becomes a fleet engagement. Faction pools, assets, and [resource moves](combat/faction.md) come online; the beat length may lengthen. Strain, conditions, positions, and clocks all **carry over untouched**.
- **Zoom in** when one thread of a big conflict becomes the story — freeze the fleet battle, play the bridge duel at Blitz speed, then zoom back out with its outcome applied (usually as conditions, edges, or a tick on the battle's clock).
- Who can hurt whom across scales is governed by one rule — see [Scale](combat/faction.md#scale).
