# Character Creation

A character in The Party is defined first by their place in the faction, and by everything else second. Creation takes about twenty minutes and produces someone already good at their job — advancement widens what you can do; it doesn't rescue you from incompetence.

Build the faction together before or alongside characters — see [Faction Overview](faction/overview.md). Characters are written into an organization, not bolted on afterward.

## The Seven Steps

### 1. Concept & Seat

Answer in one sentence each:

- **Who are you?** Ex-priest, getaway driver, ship's doctor, guild fence.
- **Which [Seat](characters/classes.md) do you hold?** Your role in the faction, which may have nothing to do with your day job.

A ship's cook can hold the Heart's Seat. The accountant can be the faction's Eye. The tension or harmony between day job and Seat is free characterization.

### 2. Seat & Calling

Choose one of the seven Seats — **Head, Mouth, Hand, Eye, Mind, Heart, Spine** — and one of its three **callings**. You get:

- your Seat's **core feature**, always on,
- your calling's **signature feature**,
- **two more features** from your Seat's list.

Two players holding the same Seat is fine and works as intended — two Hands are a formidable pair. If you double up, consider taking different callings and deciding together what history sits between you: mentor and student, rivals for the same post, siblings in the trade. Missing Seats are also fine; anyone can perform any faction function. Seat-holders just do it with sharper tools.

### 3. Stats

Assign one array across **Physical, Mental, Emotional, Ideological**:

- **Focused** 4/3/2/2 · **Broad** 4/3/3/1 · **Spiky** 4/4/2/1

### 4. Gifts & Flaw

Take **two gifts** and **one flaw** from [Gifts & Flaws](characters/traits.md). Gifts are narrow advantages; your flaw is a real mechanical weakness with a life of its own. More flaws will find you in play — [scars](core/strain-and-conditions.md#broken) are written as flaws — so start with one.

### 5. Bonds

Bonds are the connective tissue between your character, the other characters, and the faction — covered in their own section [below](characters/creation.md#writing-a-character-who-belongs).

### 6. Kit

Write a plausible loadout for who you are and where you live. Gear in The Party is **permission, not arithmetic**: a sniper rifle doesn't add dice; it lets you declare goals at Far range that a knife-holder can't. Three exceptions:

- Up to **two items** may carry a tag — a **protection tag**, or a descriptive tag like *Piercing*, *Sweeping*, *Silent*, or a range tag ([Conflict Overview](combat/overview.md#tags)). Mark these with the GM.
- A genuinely extraordinary possession — the master-forged hammer, the experimental lens — can grant a [Personal Resource edge](core/contests.md#edges-and-snags) when it's the difference-maker. Expected tools of your trade grant nothing; they're assumed.
- Genuinely scarce or faction-owned equipment (the drop ship, the printing press, the reliquary) belongs on the faction sheet as an [asset](faction/nodes.md#assets), not in your pocket.

### 7. Boxes & Perks

- **5 strain boxes.**
- **1 perk**, banked, and a lifetime tally that starts at 1 — see [Advancement](characters/advancement.md#honing).

## Writing a Character Who Belongs

The Party doesn't work with lone wolves, and it doesn't need a rule to enforce that — it needs you to build someone with reasons to stay. Before play, talk through three things with the table. Write down whatever comes of it; there are no boxes to fill and no numbers attached.

**Why you're here.** Something brought your character to this cause and keeps them at it — belief, debt, love, ambition, nowhere else to go. It doesn't have to be noble and it doesn't have to be settled; a character still deciding whether they believe is a character the GM can do wonderful things with.

**What it has cost.** Joining something means leaving something. Know what your character gave up — a home, a name, a person, a clean conscience — because the campaign will eventually offer it back at the worst possible price.

**Who these people are to you.** Look around the table. Decide, with the other players, at least one relationship that predates the campaign: who recruited you, who you'd trust with your life, who you owe, who you can't quite forgive. Characters who arrive already tangled give the story handholds from the first scene.

The GM's side of this bargain: aim at all of it. Bonds exist to be tested, costs exist to resurface, and the question of why you're here should get harder to answer as the campaign goes on, not easier.
