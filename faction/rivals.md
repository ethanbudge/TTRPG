# Rival Factions

The world is full of organizations that want what yours wants. Rivals use the same anatomy as the players' faction, compressed to what the GM actually needs at the table.

## The Rival Sheet

```
RIVAL: The Gilded Consortium
CAUSE: own every wharf from here to the delta
STATS  P 3 · M 4 · E 3 · C 2        COHESION □ □ □ □ □
NODES  Counting House (stronghold) · Harbormaster's Office · Warehouse Row
ASSETS Tariff Men (Force 3) · Ledger Spies (Intelligence 2)
GOAL CLOCK  "Strangle the small traders" ⬤⬤⬤◯◯◯
LADDER  −1 (Cold)
TAGS   patient, litigious, allergic to open violence
```

Rivals don't track stock in eight pools — that bookkeeping is for the protagonist faction only. A rival's economy is abstracted into its stats, assets, and the GM's fiction. If the players cut a rival's routes, express it as conditions (*Starved*, *Overextended*) and setbacks, exactly like [everywhere else](core/strain-and-conditions.md#conditions-at-faction-scale).

## The Ladder

Every rival (and notable neutral) sits on the **relationship ladder**:

| Position | Stance | What it means at the table |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **+3 Allied** | Bound to you | Lends stock or an asset (1 per faction turn); joins your conflicts if the cause is shared |
| **+2 Friendly** | Wants you to win | Trades 1:1; shares intelligence; warns you |
| **+1 Warm** | Well-disposed | Small favors; benefit of the doubt |
| **0 Neutral** | Indifferent | Deals at market rates |
| **−1 Cold** | Wary of you | Watches you; charges you more; talks behind your back |
| **−2 Hostile** | Wants you to lose | Spends actions against you when convenient |
| **−3 At War** | Committed | Spends actions against you *even when it hurts them* |

The ladder moves through fiction, not mechanics: broken deals, public humiliations, weddings, funerals, rescues. The GM announces shifts openly — factions telegraph their feelings through emissaries, editorials, and troop movements.

## Running Rival Turns

After each player faction turn, give each rival **one action** toward its current goal clock, then tick clocks the world advanced on its own. Guidelines:

- **One roll or none.** If a rival's action doesn't touch the players' faction, don't roll — tick their clock and narrate rumor. Roll only at points of collision.
- **Telegraph, then hit.** Rival clocks should be *visible* at 50% — informants, headlines, sermons. The players should get one faction turn of warning before most hammers fall.
- **Rivals fight each other.** Two or three rivals with incompatible causes will generate more plot than any prepared adventure. The players' faction is one wolf in a wood of wolves, not the center of a hub-and-spoke.
- **Rivals have flaws too.** Give each one exploitable dysfunction, and let the Eye find it.

## Scaling Difficulty

The rival roster is the campaign's difficulty dial — and the answer to "how do we challenge a 5-stat faction without inflating numbers":

- Early arcs: rivals with stats 2–3, sloppy assets, visible clocks.
- Later arcs: stats 4–5, layered assets, *hidden* clocks, and — most dangerous of all — rivals who cooperate against you.

A veteran faction doesn't face bigger dice; it faces smarter webs. Numbers stay small. Webs get deep.
