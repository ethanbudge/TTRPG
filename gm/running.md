# GM Guide

The GM in The Party rolls dice in every contest, runs the rival factions, and — above all — plays the world's pressure on an organization trying to grow inside it. This page is the toolkit.

## Principles

1. **Stakes before dice.** Never call for a contest without saying what a loss means. If you can't think of an interesting loss, say yes and move on. And if you can't think of an interesting win, the answer is already no — don't hide "no" inside five opposition dice.
2. **Show your pool.** Announce opposition dice and edges out loud before the roll. The drama of this game is facing known odds; hidden difficulty steals it.
3. **Ties and losses move the story.** A costly success introduces the next problem; a loss changes the situation. Neither ever means "nothing happens, try again."
4. **The faction is a character.** Its people have names, its holdings have moods, its flaw has a schedule. Route pressure through them: the Stronghold's cook asking whether the grain will come is worth three abstract warnings.
5. **Both hands on the camera.** You control the flow of time. Zoom in when a single roll deserves a whole scene; let a quiet month pass in a sentence and run the faction turn when the story exhales. Neither direction needs different rules.
6. **Let them be good.** Characters start with a 4 and win their specialty three times in four. Don't compensate with inflated pools — challenge them with dilemmas: two needs in the Keep, one wagon of grain.

## Setting Opposition

- Contested by a person or faction: their stat. Contested by the world: difficulty 1–5 ([the ladder](core/contests.md#the-opposition-pool)).
- Default to **3**. Reserve 5 for things that should feel like prying open heaven.
- Difficulty expresses the world's resistance, never "how important this roll is to the plot."
- Add opposition edges for entrenchment, alarm, and preparation — visibly, with reasons. Players who can see why a pool is big can scheme to strip it down, and that scheming is the game.

## The Stakes Menu

Vary your costs; strain is only the bluntest. A loss or tie can price out as:

**strain** (1, or 2 on a Disaster) · **a condition** · **position** (bands, cover, the initiative of events) · **resources** (stock spilled, kit lost, an asset exposed, a cache's accessibility worsened) · **time** (a countdown ticks against them) · **exposure** (someone now knows who, where, or what you are) · **a hard choice** ("you can hold the door or the ledger — not both").

## Pacing the Session

A reliable shape: open on consequence (the world moved — show a rival countdown landing), play the job or the trouble in 2–4 scenes with one real conflict, run a faction turn if time should pass, and close on the next visible dilemma. With three players, run richer scenes. With six or seven: pre-assign faction turn steps, lean on paired goals and group contests, and give major rivals a second action so the machine pushes back.

## Running the Long Game

The faction's cause needs milestones the table can see. Frame the current one as a [countdown](core/contests.md#countdowns) — *Win the Dockworkers (8)* — and when it strikes to zero, let the consequences pick the next one. You don't need a ladder planned to the throne; you need the next rung and a world that reacts when it's climbed. Rival countdowns run on the same board, at the same visible speed. When the campaign's biggest promise pays off, treat it as an era's end: the table decides together what the faction has become, and whether the next era is theirs to play.

## The Lethality Dial

The engine defaults to *consequential but survivable*: mortal stakes only when declared, and even then the player [chooses](core/strain-and-conditions.md#broken) between the scar and the exit. Say which conflicts can kill — out loud, at the start. For grittier tables, let mortal stakes be the default in violent conflicts; for pulpier ones, reserve them for finales. Declare the dial in session zero.

## Converting a Genre

The whole checklist for porting The Party to any setting:

1. **Skin the fourth stat** — faith, ideology, esprit de corps, the code ([Stats](core/stats.md)).
2. **Name the eight pools** ([Resources](faction/resources.md)) — the load-bearing step.
3. **Sketch the map** — where the faction's places, caches, and people sit in this setting's geography ([Holdings](faction/nodes.md)).
4. **Set default clockspeeds** — a heist campaign lives at Blitz and Operation; an empire game at Season and Era.
5. **Skin the Seats** — one line each: "the Mouth here is the pamphleteer-priest."
6. **Write 2–4 rivals** with visible countdowns.

No rules change.

## Table Safety

Faction play generates betrayal, fanaticism, martyrdom, and hunger as mechanics, and Crisis scenes aim at the table's own bonds. Talk before the campaign about what belongs in your game and what stays offscreen, and agree on how anyone can flag a scene that's crossed a line — no explanation owed. If your group hasn't done this before, look up *lines and veils* and the *X-card*: simple, widely used tools that take five minutes to set up. Revisit when Cohesion runs low, because that's when the game starts aiming at the things people actually care about.
