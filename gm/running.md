# GM Guide

The GM in The Party rolls dice in every contest, runs the rival factions, and — most importantly — plays the *world's pressure* on an organization trying to grow inside it. This page is the toolkit.

## Principles

1. **Stakes before dice.** Never call for a contest without saying what a loss means. If you can't think of an interesting loss, say yes and move on. (Corollary: if you can't think of an interesting *win*, the answer is already no — don't hide "no" inside 5 opposition dice.)
2. **Show your pool.** Announce opposition dice and edges out loud before the roll. The drama of The Party is *facing known odds* — the dice tell the story of going up against them. Hidden difficulty steals that.
3. **Ties and losses move the story forward.** A costly success introduces the *next* problem; a loss changes the situation — it never means "nothing happens, try again."
4. **The faction is a character — play it like one.** Its people have names, its nodes have moods, its flaw has a schedule. Route pressure through them: the Stronghold's cook asking whether the grain will come is worth three abstract shortfall warnings.
5. **Both hands on the camera.** Your most powerful tool is scale: zoom in when a single roll deserves a whole scene, zoom out when a month deserves a single roll. Neither direction needs different rules — just announce the clockspeed.
6. **Let them be good.** PCs start with a 4 and win their specialty three times in four. Don't compensate with inflated pools — challenge them with *dilemmas*: two nodes hungry, one wagon of grain.

## Setting Opposition

- Contested by a person or faction → their stat. Contested by the world → difficulty 1–5 ([ladder](core/contests.md#the-opposition-pool)).
- Default to **3**. Reserve 5 for things that should feel like prying open heaven.
- Difficulty expresses *the fiction's resistance*, never "how important is this roll to my plot."
- Add opposition edges for entrenchment, alarm, and preparation — visibly, with reasons. Players should always be able to see *why* the pool is what it is, because then they can scheme to strip it down. That scheming is the game.

## The Stakes Menu

Vary your costs; strain is only the bluntest. A loss or tie can price out as:

- **strain** (1, or 2 on bad losses) · **a condition** (the fiction's teeth) · **position** (bands, cover, initiative of the fiction) · **resources** (stock spilled, kit lost, assets exposed) · **time** (the clock ticks against them) · **exposure** (someone now knows who/where/what you are) · **a hard choice** ("you can hold the door or the ledger — not both")

## Pacing the Session

A reliable session shape: **open on consequence** (the world moved — show a rival clock's tick landing), **one venture** (2–4 scenes, one conflict), **faction turn** (20–30 min), **close on the question** (next venture's dilemma visible). With 3 players run richer ventures; with 7, run faction turns brisker (pre-assign steps) and lean on group contests so beats stay fast.

## Lethality Dial

The engine defaults to *consequential but survivable*: death only when stakes said so. For grittier tables: allow "dead" as standard taken-out stakes in violent conflicts. For pulpier ones: scars only by player choice. Declare the dial in session zero. (See [Open Questions](appendix/open-questions.md).)

## Converting a Genre

The whole checklist for porting The Party to any setting:

1. **Skin the fourth stat** — faith, ideology, esprit de corps, the code ([Stats](core/stats.md)).
2. **Name the eight pools** ([Resources](faction/resources.md)) — the load-bearing step.
3. **Sketch nodes and routes** in the setting's geography — cities, districts, stations, servers.
4. **Set default clockspeeds** — a heist campaign lives at Blitz/Operation; an empire game at Season/Era.
5. **Skin the classes** — one line each: "the Voice here is the pamphleteer-priest," etc. ([Genre frames](gm/genre-frames.md) show five worked examples.)
6. **Write 2–4 rivals** with visible clocks.

No rules change. That's the point.

## Table Safety

Faction play generates betrayal, fanaticism, martyrdom, and hunger as *mechanics*. Establish lines and veils in session zero like you would for any game that aims at the heart; revisit when the Crisis rules fire, because Crises aim at the table's own bonds.
